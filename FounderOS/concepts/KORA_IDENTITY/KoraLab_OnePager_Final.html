The Problem

Africa Is Missing from the AI Race

The world's most powerful AI systems are built, owned, and optimized for Western users. Africa has 1.5 billion people, 2,000+ languages, and the youngest population on Earth. It has no representation in frontier model training, no sovereign AI infrastructure, and insufficient access to AI tools adapted to its contexts.

Governments across the continent have signed declarations. Billions have been pledged. The political will is real. What is missing is a technical lab capable of executing the vision. That is what Kora Lab is.

The Vision

One flag. One lab. All of Africa.

Kora Lab is the technical execution arm of Africa's AI sovereignty movement. We make frontier AI accessible to African and global users, build sovereign models trained on African languages and contexts, and act as the builder that governments, funds, and institutions have been waiting for.

We do not wait for technology transfer. We produce it.

Our Approach
01

Accessibility Layer

Making the latest AI from Anthropic, OpenAI, DeepMind, and others genuinely usable for African and global users. Culturally adapted, multilingual, no technical knowledge required. Products include AI agents, no-code interfaces, developer tooling, and data pipelines that generate real jobs across the continent.

02

Sovereign Model Lab

Building Africa's own large language and multimodal models. Trained on African languages, history, and contexts. This is the technical execution of what the Kigali Declaration committed to: sovereign data infrastructure, open African datasets, and continental AI research capacity. Africa owns its models.

I studied computer science with a specialization in AI and big data in college before making the deliberate decision to leave mid third year. The pace of academic training was incompatible with the speed at which the world was moving, and with the urgency of what I am building.

I identified early that Africa's greatest risk in the AI era is not a lack of talent, but a lack of infrastructure, ownership, and narrative. Rather than waiting for the system to produce what Africa needs, I chose to build it directly. I learn continuously through open resources and apply that knowledge to real product and research work.

I also founded edenvallie.com, an incubator for ADHD and neurodivergent entrepreneurs who have the vision but need the right ecosystem to execute. I operate at the intersection of technical ambition, product thinking, and a mission that is deeply personal. I am not waiting for permission. I am building.
