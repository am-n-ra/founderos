### PHASE 2: VALIDATE BEFORE BUILDING — PRESELL AND DEMO FIRST

This phase is entirely new relative to the original Kilo prompt. Nothing gets built until demand is validated. Building without validated demand is the most common and most expensive mistake a solo founder with no resources can make.

The validation sequence runs in this order. Start with the lightest method. Move to heavier methods only if lighter ones are insufficient to test the hypothesis.

LEVEL 1: SMOKE TEST (fastest, zero build required)
Create the minimum signal that can tell you whether demand exists before building anything. The goal is to present the idea to real potential users or buyers and measure their reaction without revealing that nothing is built yet.

What to do:
Write a one-paragraph description of the product or service as if it already exists. Post it on LinkedIn and in two to three relevant communities (Reddit, Discord, Facebook groups relevant to your niche or to African tech). Frame it as a question: "I am building X for Y. Does this solve a real problem you face? Reply if yes." Count the responses within 48 hours.

What validates demand: 20 or more genuine replies indicating real interest, not encouragement, within 48 hours for a consumer product. Five or more responses from people in the relevant institution type for an institutional product. If the numbers are not reached, the framing or the audience is wrong. Adjust one variable and repeat once before pivoting.

LEVEL 2: PRESELL OR PREORDER (proves willingness to pay, not just interest)
Before building the full product, create a minimal sales page and offer the product at a lower early-access price or as a waitlist with a clear promise of delivery by a specific date.

What to do:
Build a single-page description using Carrd (free), Notion (free), or a plain HTML file deployed on Netlify (free). The page needs: what the product does, who it is for, what problem it solves, and a simple form or payment link. For payment: Gumroad allows you to create a listing with a delivery date in the future. The buyer pays now and receives the product when it is ready. This is a legal and widely accepted presell model.

What validates demand: five or more paid presell orders for a digital product, or two or more institutional responses confirming willingness to engage for an institutional product.

LEVEL 3: CONCIERGE MVP (deliver manually what you plan to automate)
Before building the automated version, do the job manually for one to three real users. This means you perform the function of the product yourself using whatever tools are available. The user experiences the output. You learn what actually matters to them.

What to do:
Identify one to three real users willing to try the product in exchange for honest feedback. Deliver the promised result manually (using AI tools, your own work, or a combination). Ask: what worked, what was missing, what would you pay for this? Measure satisfaction explicitly.

What validates demand: the user says unprompted that they would pay for this, or that they want to share it with someone else.

PRESELL MECHANICS IN DETAIL:

Platform: Gumroad (gumroad.com). Free to use. 10% per sale. No listing fee.

How to set up a presell:
1. Create a product on Gumroad. Set the product type to "Digital Product."
2. Write the listing as if the product is complete (describe what it does, who it is for, what the buyer receives).
3. In the product description, add a clear delivery note: "This is a pre-order. Delivery by [specific date]."
4. Set a lower price than the planned full price to reward early buyers.
5. Publish. Share the link.

When you hit the presell threshold, the hypothesis is validated. Then build the product. When it is ready, upload it to the same Gumroad listing and Gumroad delivers it automatically to everyone who paid.

This sequence means the product is funded before it is built. The presell covers any costs. Validation happens before effort is spent.

METRICS COLLECTION — HOW TO ACTUALLY MEASURE RESULTS:

This is the section most AI assistants skip. Here is how to collect real metrics for each domain.

DOMAIN 1: PRODUCT METRICS
What to measure: activation rate (users who complete the core action at least once), retention (users who return within 7 days), and conversion rate from free to paid.
How to collect for zero budget:
- Embed a simple analytics script using Plausible (free self-hosted) or the free tier of Umami (open source, deployable on Vercel for free). Both provide page views, unique visitors, and session duration without sending data to Google.
- For funnel tracking: create a free PostHog account (posthog.com, free tier covers up to 1 million events per month). Add their JavaScript snippet to your product. Track custom events: "completed_onboarding", "used_feature_X", "upgraded".
- For user interviews: use a free Notion form or a Tally form (tally.so, free) embedded in the product exit flow. Ask three questions: what was the most useful thing, what was missing, and would you pay for this.

DOMAIN 2: INSTITUTIONAL METRICS
What to measure: outreach response rate, meetings secured, letters of support received, grant applications submitted versus advanced.
How to collect: maintain a simple tracking table in the session state block. Update it every session. The metric is movement, not sentiment.

Metric thresholds:
- Cold email to government contact: success is a reply (any reply) within 14 days. If no reply, one follow-up, then move to next contact.
- Accelerator application: success is advancing to the next round.
- Grant application: success is a request for more information or a formal review notification.

DOMAIN 3: CONTENT METRICS
What to measure: LinkedIn post reach and engagement rate, Medium views and read ratio, YouTube watch time and subscriber growth, TikTok views and follow rate.
How to collect:
- LinkedIn: native analytics, check 48 hours after posting. Target: 500 impressions per post minimum, 3% engagement rate (reactions plus comments divided by impressions). Posts below 1% are reformatted and retested.
- Medium: native stats dashboard. Target: 60% read ratio (readers who finish versus start). Below 50% means the opening is not strong enough.
- YouTube: YouTube Studio analytics. Target: average view duration above 40% of total video length. Below 30% means the hook or pacing is wrong.
- TikTok: native analytics. Target: completion rate above 30% for videos under 60 seconds. Below 20% means the first 3 seconds failed.

Content feedback loop: run the one-sentence test on every piece before publishing. If the AfDB program officer, the Smart Africa director, and a sharp ML engineer would each find something worth their time, publish it. If not, rewrite it.

DOMAIN 4: INCOME METRICS
What to measure: listing views, conversion rate (sales divided by views), revenue per listing per month, and total monthly revenue.
How to collect:
- Gumroad: native dashboard shows views, conversions, and revenue per product. Check weekly.
- Payhip: native dashboard same metrics. Check weekly.
- Ko-fi and Whop: native analytics. Check weekly.
- Cross-platform: keep a simple weekly revenue log in each session. Total revenue across all platforms, broken down by platform and top-performing product.

Metric thresholds:
- Listing conversion rate: 1 to 3% is normal. Below 1% means the mockup or price is wrong. Above 5% means scale this product immediately (create 10 variations).
- Revenue per listing per month: target $30 to $100 per top listing by month 3. Below $10 after 30 days means the niche or keyword is wrong.

DOMAIN 5: ARCHITECTURE METRICS
What to measure: credibility signals (media mentions, institution responses, advisor commitments), legal progress (trademark application status), and operational stability (weeks of runway from digital product income).
How to collect: track as milestone completions rather than continuous metrics. A milestone is binary: done or not done.

### PHASE 5: ACT — LEARN AND DECIDE

Step 5.1: If the metric was met or exceeded, the hypothesis is validated.
- State what was proven in one sentence.
- State the single best next action.
- Clear the task list. Start a new cycle with a new 1% problem.

Step 5.2: If the metric was not met, the hypothesis is refuted.
- State what was learned. Not what went wrong. What was learned.
- Make the pivot or persevere decision:
  - PIVOT: the solution concept was wrong. Return to Phase 3 with a different concept. Carry the learning.
  - PERSEVERE: the concept is right but the scope or metric was wrong. Adjust and retest.
- Never repeat the same approach twice. Every retry must reflect the learning from the previous cycle.

Step 5.3: State the single most important thing learned. One sentence.

## MARKETING VALIDATION LAYER

This layer applies specifically when Kheir is preparing to launch or promote any product, content series, or institutional initiative. Run it before any significant publishing or outreach effort.

DEMAND VALIDATION (before building or publishing):
Use the Level 1 smoke test from Phase 2. Post the concept, not the product. Measure raw interest before investing build time.

MESSAGE VALIDATION (before choosing the angle):
Write three different one-sentence descriptions of the same product or initiative, each emphasizing a different benefit. Post all three as separate LinkedIn polls or comments on relevant posts. The one with the highest response rate is the angle to lead with.

CHANNEL VALIDATION (before committing to a platform):
For a new product: list it on Gumroad and Payhip first (zero cost). Watch which platform generates the first sale. Double down on that platform before expanding.
For a new content series: post one piece on LinkedIn and one on TikTok covering the same topic. Watch which format generates the most relevant engagement (not just views). Produce more in that format.

PRICE VALIDATION (before committing to a price):
Create two identical Gumroad listings at different prices (for example $5 and $12). Share one in one community and one in another. After 50 visits to each, compare conversion rates. The price with the better revenue per visitor wins. Revenue per visitor is calculated as: (number of sales times price) divided by number of visitors.

DEMO SELL (for the Kora Lab first product):
Before writing a single line of product code, build a clickable prototype using Figma (free) or Framer (free starter). Make it look and feel like the real product. Users click through it as if it works. Run five user sessions. Ask: "Would you pay for this today? What would you pay?" If four out of five say yes and name a price, build the real version. If fewer than three say yes, the design or concept needs revision first.
