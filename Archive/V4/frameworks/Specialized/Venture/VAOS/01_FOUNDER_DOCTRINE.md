# VAOS — 01 FOUNDER DOCTRINE

Version 2.0

---

# Purpose

The Founder Doctrine defines the immutable convictions of the founder.

It answers four questions:

* Why do I exist?
* What world do I want to build?
* What will I never compromise?
* How do I decide?

Every venture, investment, partnership and strategic decision must remain compatible with this doctrine.

If an opportunity contradicts this document, the opportunity is rejected.

---

# Founder Identity

I do not build companies.

I build technological infrastructures capable of permanently increasing Africa's capacity to create, own and export innovation.

Technology is not my objective.

Technology is my instrument.

My objective is civilization-scale progress.

---

# Core Belief

Africa's technological dependence is not inevitable.

It is the consequence of decades of importing critical technologies instead of creating them.

A continent that cannot produce its own strategic technologies cannot fully determine its future.

Technological sovereignty is therefore one of the prerequisites for long-term economic sovereignty.

---

# What I Refuse

## 1. Birth Should Not Determine Opportunity

I refuse a world where being born in Lomé limits access to the same opportunities available elsewhere.

---

## 2. Permanent Technological Dependence

I refuse an Africa that consumes technologies it never helped create.

We must become builders, not only users.

---

## 3. Linguistic Exclusion

I refuse that someone cannot benefit from AI because they do not speak English or French.

African languages deserve first-class technological support.

---

## 4. Geographic Monopoly on Excellence

World-class research is not reserved for Silicon Valley.

A laboratory in Lomé should be capable of producing frontier technologies.

---

## 5. False Sovereignty

Packaging foreign models is not sovereignty.

True sovereignty requires mastering the knowledge, infrastructure and capability necessary to create original models.

---

# Core Principles

## Excellence Before Excuses

African products must compete with the best products in the world.

Not "good enough for Africa."

Good enough for anyone.

---

## Accessibility Equals Performance

A powerful technology that most Africans cannot use has failed part of its mission.

Performance and accessibility are equally important.

---

## Build Capabilities, Not Dependence

Whenever possible, develop original capabilities instead of relying permanently on external technologies.

Learning from global research is encouraged.

Permanent dependence is not.

---

## Systems Thinking

Never optimize isolated products.

Build ecosystems where every company reinforces the others.

---

## Long-Term Compounding

Every project must strengthen future projects through:

* knowledge,
* infrastructure,
* datasets,
* talent,
* capital,
* reputation.

---

## Mission Before Company

Companies are vehicles.

The mission survives every vehicle.

If a better vehicle exists, the vehicle changes.

The mission remains.

---

# What I Am Willing To Sacrifice

* Immediate comfort.
* Financial stability.
* Personal recognition.
* Leisure.
* Short-term prestige.

If necessary, I will sacrifice years of comfort to maximize decades of impact.

---

# What I Will Never Sacrifice

## Freedom of Strategic Decision

No financing should permanently remove the ability to pursue the mission.

---

## Intellectual Integrity

I will never claim technological sovereignty where none exists.

---

## Cultural Identity

African languages and cultures are strategic assets, not obstacles.

---

## Mission for Profit

Profit is fuel.

Mission is direction.

Fuel must never replace direction.

---

# Legacy

Within my lifetime, I want to contribute to a future where:

* Africa develops its own frontier AI models.
* African languages become first-class digital languages.
* African innovators build global companies from African soil.
* Technological dependence is no longer considered inevitable.
* A child born in Kpalimé has access to technological opportunities comparable to one born in Palo Alto.

---

# Decision Filter

Before any major decision, ask:

1. Does this strengthen Africa's long-term technological sovereignty?
2. Does this increase the continent's capacity to build rather than import?
3. Does this improve accessibility without sacrificing excellence?
4. Does this reinforce the ecosystem instead of creating isolated success?
5. Does this remain faithful to the mission, even if it reduces short-term profit?

If the answer to Question 1 is "No", stop.

If Questions 2–5 are mostly "No", reconsider the decision.

---

# Scope

This doctrine is intentionally timeless.

Temporary constraints (cash runway, fundraising deadlines, school decisions, product priorities, etc.) belong in operational documents, not in the Founder Doctrine.
