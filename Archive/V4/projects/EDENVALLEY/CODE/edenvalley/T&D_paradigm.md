# PART I: THINKERS (Visionaries, Strategists)

## 1. Definition

A **Thinker** in startup terminology is a founder type characterized by strategic, long-term thinking; comfort with ambiguity; pattern recognition across markets; and non-conviction based on first principles reasoning.

**Aliases:** Visionary, Strategist, Conceptual Founder, Architect

### Key Archetypes
- **First Principled Thinker:** Reduces problems to fundamental truths, reasoning independently of precedent
- **Reality-Bender:** Sees different odds than everyone else, processes information to reach fundamentally different conclusions

---

## 2. Psychological Traits & Cognitive Patterns

### Big Five Personality Research (Nature, 2023 - 21,187 startups)

| Trait | Description | Manifestation in Startups |
|-------|-------------|-------------------------|
| **Openness to Adventure** | Preference for variety, novelty | Identifies non-obvious opportunities |
| **Lower Modesty** | Comfort being center of attention | Evangelizes vision compellingly |
| **Higher Activity/Energy** | Exuberant, high-intensity | Drives momentum and urgency |
| **Low Neuroticism** | Emotional stability under stress | Resilience through uncertainty |
| **Low Agreeableness** | Willingness to challenge | Holds difficult positions |

### Cognitive Patterns
- **System 2 Thinking:** Slow, deliberative, analytical
- **Counterfactual Reasoning:** "What if?" thinking sharpens problem-solving
- **Metacognition:** Thinking about one's own thinking
- **Strategic Persistence:** Holding conviction through setbacks

### Strengths & Weaknesses

**Thinker Strengths:**
- Vision creation and articulation
- Identifying non-obvious opportunities
- Strategic persistence through setbacks
- Attracting talent and investment
- Synthesizing complex market signals
- Comfort with ambiguity

**Thinker Weaknesses:**
- Execution details and process design
- Information-seeking behaviors (can neglect customer discovery)
- Customer service orientation gaps
- Flexibility can become scope creep
- Prone to "vision tunnel vision"
- Delegation challenges

## 3. Problems Thinkers Face

### The Vision-Execution Gap
- **67% of well-articulated strategies fail due to execution issues** (Harvard Business Review)
- Thinkers often cannot execute what they can clearly envision

### Founder Bottleneck
- Strategy lives only in founder's head
- Teams execute in "slightly different directions"
- Only 28% of managers can identify their company's strategy (MIT Sloan)
- 95% of employees don't understand the strategy
- Growth gets capped at one person's bandwidth

### The Founder's Paradox
- Vision that starts a company can also blind it
- Same traits that make Thinkers effective become liabilities post-product-market-fit

**Three Balancing Acts:**
1. **Confidence vs. Coachability:** Unshakable belief + willingness to change
2. **Speed vs. Sustainability:** Urgency + thoughtful pacing
3. **Dream vs. Data:** Vision + evidence-based iteration

### Competency Deficits (35 of 50 failed startups - Frontiers in Psychology 2024)
- Information-seeking deficit: 35/50
- Customer service orientation gap: 33/50

# PART II: DOERS (Executors, Operators)

## 1. Definition

A **Doer** is a founder or team member whose primary role is to translate ideas and visions into tangible, operational outcomes. They are the execution arm of a founding team.

**Aliases:** Executor, Operator, Builder, Technical Co-Founder, Hacker, Maker

### Key Archetypes
- **Operator:** Highest in conscientiousness, orderliness; excels at structured execution
- **Developer:** Technical builder focused on implementation
- **Hacker:** Building from first principles in code

---

## 2. Psychological Traits & Cognitive Patterns

### Big Five Personality Profile

| Trait | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **High Conscientiousness** | Strong organizational skills, discipline, reliability |
| **High Emotional Stability** | Resilience under pressure, calm in chaos |
| **Lower Neuroticism** | Less anxiety, better stress handling |
| **Lower Openness** | Less preference for novelty-seeking (relative) |
| **Lower Modesty** | Comfort with visibility in execution |

### Cognitive Patterns

| Pattern | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Execution-Focused Cognition** | Thrive on "how" questions |
| **Preference for Clarity** | Excel with clear briefs and defined constraints |
| **Architectural Thinking** | Comfort with complexity |
| **Sustained Focus** | Concentration over long build cycles |
| **Need for Cognition** | Engineers score higher than average |

### Strengths & Weaknesses

**Doer Strengths:**
- Shipping discipline: Get products out the door
- Technical depth: Critical technical decisions daily
- Reliability: Execute without supervision
- Problem-solving: Find creative ways to complete tasks
- Product ownership: Connect user needs with technology
- Adaptability: Pivot and iterate when things go wrong

**Doer Weaknesses:**
- Vision blindness: Immersed in execution, miss bigger picture
- Direction dependency: Procrastinate when asked to generate briefs
- Risk aversion: May resist bold pivots
- Under-investment in strategy: Focus on immediate over long-term
- Perfectionism: Over-engineer rather than ship MVP

## 3. Problems Doers Face

### Common Challenges

**A. The Matching Problem**
- Non-technical founders underestimate product clarity needed
- Technical doers underestimate distribution difficulty
- **65% of startups fail due to co-founder conflict** (Harvard/Noam Wasserman)
- 21% of startups fail due to team issues

**B. Role Confusion**
- Assumption both founders should do both visionary + executor roles
- Confusion between "co-founder" and "contractor" expectations
- Mismatch in full-time vs. "nights and weekends" commitment

**C. The "Implementation Factory" Fallacy**
- Non-technical partners view engineering as "just build it"
- Short-term client requests vs. long-term architecture
- Technical debt accumulates silently (50%+ efficiency loss)

**D. Single Point of Failure**
- One founder becomes "gatekeeper" of all technology
- Bus factor of 1 creates key-person risk
- **Valuation discounts of 30-50% due to key-person dependency**

**E. Communication Breakdown**
- Technical jargon gap
- "API endpoints" vs. "LTV and CAC"
- Product vs. market priority conflicts

**F. Equity and Commitment Mismatches**
- Unclear equity splits create resentment
- Misaligned time commitment
- "Passive co-founder" who only attends evening meetings

# PART III: THINKER-DOER RELATIONSHIPS

## 1. How They Work Together

### The Mechanics of Successful Partnership

The Thinker-Doer partnership works when each party brings genuinely complementary cognitive profiles and accepts that their role is not different but structurally opposed in valuable ways:

- **Thinker:** Comfort with ambiguity, pattern recognition, holding multiple possible futures
- **Doer:** Comfort with closure, architectural thinking, discipline to ship

When these orientations align around shared purpose:
**Thinker** defines destination → **Doer** charts route → both navigate

### Critical Success Factors
1. **Acknowledgment:** Thinker's output = direction, Doer's output = delivery (functional division)
2. **Translation rituals:** Regular sessions where vision becomes roadmap
3. **Ego management protocols:** Agreements about override rights in each domain
4. **Mutual respect:** For the difficulty and value of each function

### The Key Insight
The partnership does NOT require both individuals to be exclusively Thinker or Doer. What matters is that **one person is clearly the primary authority on direction** and **the other is clearly the primary authority on execution** — each within their domain.

## 2. What Goes Wrong: Failure Modes

### Composition-Specific Failures

| Combination | Failure Mode | Warning Signs |
|-------------|--------------|---------------|
| **Visionary + Visionary** | Direction conflict | Debates produce no decisions |
| **Visionary + Executor** | Execution alignment or trust breakdown | Scope creep or autonomy friction |
| **Executor + Executor** | Drift | Ships but no coherent goal |

### Structural Failure Modes
1. **Translation failure:** Vision never concretized; execution feedback never informs strategy
2. **Escalation failure:** Disagreements become personality conflicts
3. **Evolution failure:** Roles that worked at idea stage misalign at growth stage

### Emotional Patterns That Predict Failure
- Avoidance masquerading as harmony
- Asymmetric commitment (one all-in, one with backup plan)
- Scorekeeping vs. shared ownership
- Inability to repair after conflict

## 3. Co-Founder Conflict Causes

### Research (Noam Wasserman, Harvard Business School)

**65% of high-potential startups fail due to problems between people at the top** — not product, market, or fundraising.

### Categories of Conflict

| Cause | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Unequal Commitment** | One working weekends, other maintains normal hours |
| **Different Success Definitions** | Venture-backed vs. lifestyle business |
| **Unclear Roles** | Who makes calls on product, customers, hiring |
| **Equity Conversation** | Poor initial allocation |

### Deeper Causes (Psychological Fault Lines)
1. **Power:** Who gets final say, who sets direction
2. **Recognition:** One becomes public face, other feels invisible
3. **Closeness:** Like marriages without institutional support
4. **Language:** Process disagreement differently (data vs. intuition)

### Escalation Patterns
1. Specific decision → roles → legitimacy → identity
2. Conflict spikes at: **pre-seed, seed, Series A** (growth thresholds)

## 4. Successful Pairs Structure

### Best Practices

1. **Roles in Ownership Terms**
   - "You own marketing, I'll own product" = ownership
   - Ownership = responsible for outcome + authority to decide + accountable for results

2. **Decision-Making Authority Map**
   - One person = CEO (breaks ties)
   - Company-level decisions → designated
   - Clear thresholds for unanimous consent vs. one-person approval

3. **Explicit Documentation**
   - Roles, equity, vesting, decision rights
   - What happens if someone leaves
   - How disputes resolved

4. **Rituals for Alignment**
   - Weekly "founder syncs" on relationship (not just operations)
   - Quarterly strategic offsites
   - Annual reviews of roles/decision rights

5. **Curiosity About Each Other's Function**
   - Thinker asks Doer about technical challenges
   - Doer asks Thinker about market observations
   - Build genuine appreciation

## 5. Equity Splits, Roles, Decision-Making

### Equity Split Best Practices

**NOT** to do:
- Avoid conversation
- Default to 50-50 for harmony

**TO** do:
- Have conversation early (before incorporation)
- Base allocation on explicit factors:
  - Full-time vs. part-time commitment
  - Opportunity cost
  - Role criticality
  - Capital contributions
  - Network access
  - Founder-market fit
- Document rationale

**Data:** 45.9% of 2-person teams now use 50-50 splits (up from 31.5% in 2015). Equal doesn't always mean fair.

### Vesting
**Standard:** 4 years with 1-year cliff
- Before 1 year: 0%
- After 1 year: 25%
- Monthly thereafter: ~2.08%

### Decision-Making

**The most dangerous sentence:** "We'll decide together" → produces deadlock

**Best practice:**
- Name CEO early
- Full authority by domain
- What needs unanimous consent vs. one-person approval
- RACI (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) or DACI

## 6. Who Wins in Conflicts

### Research Findings

| Pattern | Winner |
|---------|--------|
| **Default** | Whoever cares more about the decision |
| **Established authority** | Historically responsible party |
| **No structures** | Politics, persuasion, silent resentment |

### Escalation Path
1. Specific decision → role conflict → legitimacy conflict → identity conflict
2. **Intervene early** at specific decision level

### Conflicts as Proxies
- Equity/compensation disputes → often about fairness, recognition, power

### Key Finding
> **Conflicts are won or lost before they're resolved.** Founders who survive conflicts established clear structures before conflict—not those better at argument.

The answer to "who wins" is whoever built better infrastructure while things were going well.

## 7. Frameworks for Collaboration

### The Founder Agreement (10 Items)

1. **Dispute path:** Internal escalation → mediation → arbitration
2. **Performance remedies:** Scope reset, removal process
3. **Governance:** Board setup, thresholds
4. **Leaving:** Good vs. bad leaver, repurchase, price
5. **IP ownership:** Code, designs, data, domains to company
6. **Cash vs. sweat:** Salaries, expense policy, loan repayment
7. **Time commitment:** Full-time date, side gigs, availability
8. **Decision rights:** CEO, break ties, unanimous consent
9. **Vesting:** 4 years, cliff, monthly
10. **Equity split:** With written rationale

### The Decision Scorecard

Score each option on:
- **Impact:** How much does this move the main outcome?
- **Urgency:** What breaks if not done in 90 days?
- **Effort:** How much work does this create?
- **Confidence:** How sure will this work?
- **Risk:** What could go wrong?
- **Focus fit:** Match current priorities?

### Reflect-Repair-Recommit Cycle

1. **Reflect (within 24h):** Self-audit—what did I want, signal?
2. **Repair:** Acknowledge contribution, understand other perspective
3. **Recommit:** Affirm partnership commitment + next steps

### The DRl Model (Directly Responsible Individual)

For each decision:
- **R**esponsible: Makes the call
- **A**ccountable: Has final authority
- **C**onsulted: Provides input
- **I**nformed: Knows outcome

### The Big Decision Framework

Answer:
- **WHY:** What feels big, what's at stake
- **WHAT:** Options, including doing nothing
- **WHO:** Decides, affected, expertise
- **WHEN:** Timeframe
- **HOW:** Next steps
- **WHAT:** Risk

### Escalation Ladders with SLAs

1. Debate async (24 hours)
2. Escalate to directly responsible individual (24 hours)
3. Escalate to board advisor/mediator

### Other Frameworks

- **Slicing Pie Model:** Dynamic equity based on actual contributions (complex - investors dislike)
- **Six Thinking Hats:** Facts, Emotions, Risks, Benefits, Creativity, Process
- **The Ensemble Theory:** Diverse personality teams outperform homogeneous ones by 16%

# PART V: SCIENTIFIC FOUNDATION

## 1. Validated Constructs

### Action vs. State Orientation (STRONGEST)

** Julius Kuhl's Action Control Theory:**
- **Action Orientation (AO):** Ability to initiate and maintain goal-directed behavior
- **State Orientation (SO):** Tendency toward cognitive rumination; difficulty initiating action

**Empirical Evidence:**
- AO predicts better conflict adaptation (Fischer, 2015)
- AO associated with better self-regulation under stress (Jostmann & Koole, 2010)
- Moderates relationship between executive function and real-life self-control (Wolff, 2016)

**Measure:** Action Control Scale (ACS-90)

### Executive Function (STRONG)

**Miyake's Three-Factor Model (2000):**
1. **Inhibition:** Suppressing prepotent responses
2. **Updating:** Maintaining/manipulating working memory
3. **Shifting:** Mental set switching

**Measure:** D-KEFS, BRIEF

### Big Five (VERY STRONG)

**Meta-Analytic Findings:**
- Entrepreneurs higher in Openness and Conscientiousness, lower in Neuroticism and Agreeableness (Zhao & Seibert, 2006)
- Extraversion, Openness, Conscientiousness → entrepreneurial intention (Brandstätter, 2011)

**Entrepreneurial Profile:**
- High Extraversion + Openness + Conscientiousness + Emotional Stability
- Low Agreeableness

### Adaptors vs. Innovators (MODERATE)

**Kirton's A-I Theory:**
- **Adaptors:** Prefer structured environments, incremental improvements
- **Innovators:** Prefer unstructured settings, radical change

**Measure:** Kirton Adaption-Innovation Inventory (KAI)

## Current State Summary

| Group | Problem |

> **The problem is NOT finding each other. The problem is SURVIVING the relationship.**

This is the insight: even perfect matches fail because there's no framework for roles, equity, decision-making, and accountability.

### For Doers (UNCLAIMED):

The **unclaimed vulnerability** for Doers that could create the AHA moment:

> **Doers are tired of being treated like replaceable labor while being blamed for decisions they never made.**
