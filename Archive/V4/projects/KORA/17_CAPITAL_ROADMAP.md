# KORA LAB — 17. CAPITAL ROADMAP

**Version:** 1.0

---

# Purpose

The Capital Roadmap defines how Kora Lab will finance its mission from inception to institutional permanence.

Capital is not the objective.

Capital is the fuel that accelerates capability.

Every financing decision should strengthen Kora's long-term sovereignty rather than weaken it.

The Capital Roadmap therefore answers:

* How much capital is needed?
* When is it needed?
* Why is it needed?
* From whom should it be accepted?
* Under which conditions should it be refused?

---

# Capital Philosophy

Kora does not raise money because fundraising is fashionable.

Kora raises capital only when additional capital creates additional capability.

Capital should purchase:

* time;
* talent;
* compute;
* research;
* infrastructure;
* distribution.

Capital should never purchase vanity.

---

# Capital Principles

## Principle 1

Mission before Money.

No investment should compromise the Mission.

---

## Principle 2

Capability before Valuation.

A higher valuation without stronger capability creates fragility.

---

## Principle 3

Revenue before Dependence.

Whenever possible,

Kora should finance growth through products before relying on investors.

---

## Principle 4

Strategic Investors before Financial Investors.

The best capital brings:

knowledge,

relationships,

compute,

customers,

credibility.

Not only cash.

---

## Principle 5

Sovereignty before Speed.

Fast money that compromises independence should be rejected.

---

# Financing Architecture

Capital should evolve alongside Kora's maturity.

---

# Phase I

## Founder Capital

### Objective

Survive.

At this stage,

the company finances itself through founder effort.

Sources include:

* consulting;
* software services;
* AI deployments;
* APIs;
* subscriptions;
* early products;
* grants;
* competitions.

Priority:

Runway.

Not valuation.

---

## Expected Use

* food;
* internet;
* housing;
* essential operations;
* first compute;
* prototypes;
* initial experiments.

---

# Phase II

## Non-Dilutive Capital

Objective:

Increase research capability without reducing ownership.

Priority sources:

Government grants

Innovation funds

Research programs

Cloud credits

Compute sponsorships

Academic collaborations

Technology partnerships

Challenge prizes

Philanthropic funding

Every dollar raised should preserve equity whenever possible.

---

# Phase III

## Strategic Revenue

Objective:

Allow products to finance research.

Priority revenue sources:

Enterprise AI

Government deployments

APIs

Developer platform

Speech services

Education

Custom AI solutions

Infrastructure licensing

Revenue becomes the primary engine of independence.

---

# Phase IV

## Strategic Equity

Only after proving capability,

products,

and market relevance.

Potential investors:

Mission-aligned VC

Strategic corporations

Sovereign funds

Development finance institutions

Family offices

Research-oriented investors

Selection criteria:

Do they accelerate the Mission?

Not:

How large is the check?

---

# Phase V

## Institutional Capital

Once Kora becomes an institution,

capital shifts toward permanence.

Examples:

Research endowment

Permanent compute fund

Foundation support

Long-term sovereign partnerships

Institutional research funding

The objective becomes continuity,

not growth.

---

# Capital Allocation

Every unit of capital should reinforce strategic assets.

Target allocation philosophy:

35% Research

25% Compute Infrastructure

15% Engineering

10% Product Development

10% Distribution

5% Operations

This distribution evolves over time,

but research and infrastructure remain dominant.

---

# What Kora Refuses

Kora will not pursue capital that requires:

Mission drift.

Technological dependence.

False sovereignty.

Research secrecy imposed by investors.

Control over strategic decisions.

Short-term optimization at the expense of long-term capability.

---

# Capital Decision Framework

Before accepting any funding,

answer:

1. Does this capital increase capability?
2. Does it increase sovereignty?
3. Does it preserve strategic freedom?
4. Does it accelerate the current phase?
5. Could we obtain the same outcome through revenue instead?
6. What obligations accompany this money?
7. What happens if this investor leaves tomorrow?

Capital that weakens independence should usually be refused.

# Risks

The greatest capital risks are not insufficient money.

They are:

* raising too early;
* raising from the wrong partners;
* optimizing valuation instead of capability;
* becoming dependent on continuous fundraising;
* sacrificing strategic control.

---

# Decision Filter

Before pursuing any financing opportunity:

1. Which phase of the Capital Roadmap does this belong to?
2. Does this strengthen long-term capability?
3. Does this preserve Kora's strategic independence?
4. Is this the least expensive form of capital available?
5. Would we still make this decision if the check were twice as large?

If the Mission becomes weaker because of the money,

the money should not be accepted.
